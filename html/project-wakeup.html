Wake Up – Three Thing Game (May 2018)

The Three Thing Game is a 24-hour Game Jam, hosted by the Computer Science department at the University of Hull. It takes place twice a year. Teams are given three random words which are to be somehow implemented into their game.

My team consisted of 4 people: Anthony Beacock (myself), Jakob Scrivener, Dean Barnard and Reece Giles (who stayed for the Friday night), and our words were “Hopping”, “Sheeple” and “Building”.

Team Member Role(s)
Tony (myself) Programming
Jake Programming and Art
Dean Programming
Reece Programming

After some thought, we decided on creating a game called “Wake Up!”, in which the main character is a sheep that does not conform to his stereotype. The term “Sheeple” referred to people who follow the crowd (like sheep) and aren’t individual in any way.

The first part of the game would be a 2D side-scroller platform type game, in which the character has to “hop” from building to building, whilst being chased by the Sheeple. There would also be various “sheeple” stood on some of the rooftops, waiting to kill you. In order to continue unharmed, one must shoot projectiles at them. These projectiles are “Bah’s” which suggests that the character is “Spitting Bah’s”… (Pun Intended!).

The levels were tile based. They were initially created by Reece and then later tweaked by Jake. An attempt was made to match the required “hops” between buildings, to the music that a friend of the team (Kieren Goodwin-Green) had made.

The mechanics behind the boss level were created by myself. I combined these with the artwork that Jake had previously created.

Most of the Boss’ movement consisted of sine waves. One controlled the horizontal movement of the whole body, making it move left and right. Each arm was also controlled by its own sin wave, which made both of them move separately and vertically. This gave the illusion that the boss was more intelligent than it actually was. This very basic AI also made the boss a little more unpredictable, as it would be different every time a user played the game. Each arm had a health of 25 hits, which meant that one would have to aim and attack more individual parts of the boss’ body, rather than shooting aimlessly.

Each sin wave used in the game was limited by using variables such as magnitude and frequency. This allowed me to adjust the speed and distance of each object the sin waves controlled.

The creation of this game challenged various skills, both general and technical. Some of the more general of these included creativity, teamwork, working under pressure. Some of the more technical skills were the use of the Unity Engine and Programming in C# language.

As always, I enjoyed the Three Thing Game this year, as it was a great opportunity to improve and strengthen skills, whilst having fun in a laid back, friendly but competitive environment.
